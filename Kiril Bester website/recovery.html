WHAT ARE THE MOST COMMON INJURIES?

1) Epicondylitis (Tennis Elbow)

Epicondylitis is inflammation of the tendons of your elbow. Excessive flexion and extension of your wrist can cause tiny tears in the tissues of the tendons. There are two types of epicondylitis: Medial epicondylitis affects the inside of your elbow, while lateral epicondylitis occurs on the outside.





2) Shin Splints

Constant sports activity or running can cause pain within the shinbone. Shin splints is knowns as medial tibial stress syndrome in the medical profession. It commonly happens when there is a sudden change in a workout, meaning that there needs to be a moderate change in the workout routine.





3) Hamstring Strains

Motions that can lead you to falling forwards or kicking your leg out suddenly and sharply can lead to a hamstring strain. Hamstring refers to the three muscles at the back of the thigh.








4) Knee Injuries

Also known as Patellofemoral Syndrome in the medical profession. This occurs when multiple tissues sustain the movement of the kneecap against the end of the thighbone. The activities that lead to the damage of the tissues are jumping and running.



5) Ankle Sprain

In general a humans ligaments that are outside the ankle are weak. If the ankle twists, it can lead to the ligaments taering or stretching. Ankle sprains can be very painful, depending on the amount of strain, but you should immediately see a doctor to make sure the ankle isn't fractured.




TREATMENT FOR THESE INJURIES

1) Epicondylitis (Tennis Elbow)

Treatment for Epicondylitis is usually letting your elbow rest. If the medication that has been prescribed, to you, is not working then the doctor can suggest physical therapy.

If the symptoms are related to tennis, the doctor may need to see your swings, to determine the best ways to prevent stress on the injury. Using a brace can reduce the stress. A physical therapist can teach you exercises to gradually stretch and strengthen your muscles, especially the muscles of your forearm. Eccentric exercises, which involve lowering your wrist very slowly after raising it, are particularly helpful.

2) Shin Splints

Treatment for shin splints is usually conservative, consisting of ice, over-the-counter pain medication, and rest. If pain persists, you should see a doctor to rule out a stress fracture.

Rest your body: Your body needs time to rest/heal. Ice your shin to ease pain and swelling: Do it for 20-30 minutes every 3 to 4 hours for 2 to 3 days, or until the pain is gone. If the pain is excessive and occrs frequently, take anti-inflammatory painkillers.

3) Hamstring Strains

Treatment for a hamstring strain often consists of RICE treatment at first. It can progress to physical therapy to improve the strength and flexibility of the hamstring once the initial swelling and pain have resolved.

Stop putting strain or activities that will make the pan worse, until the pain is gone. Ice the area about 24 to 48 hours after your injury. Keep the ice on for 15 minutes, take it away for 15 minutes and repeat. Sit on a table with your injured leg hanging off. Gently raise and lower your leg during icing period. After icing, lie in a prone position on your stomach and continue to gently bend and straighten your leg. If the hamstring pain is tto much, then it would be best to use crutches to reduce unnecessary strain.

4) Knee Injuries

Orthotic shoe inserts that take pressure off your knee joint can help, as can physical therapy to strengthen the quadriceps. Healing patellofemoral syndrome can take up to six weeks. Strengthening the muscles around your knee will make it more stable.

Your doctor may recommend physical therapy or different types of strengthening exercises based on the specific condition that is causing your pain. If you are physically active or practice a sport, you may need exercises to correct movement patterns that may be affecting your knees and to establish good technique during your sport or activity. Exercises to improve your flexibility and balance also are important. Arch supports, sometimes with wedges on one side of the heel, can help shift pressure away from the side of the knee most affected by osteoarthritis. In certain conditions, different types of braces may be used to help protect and support the knee joint.

5) Ankle Sprain

Physical therapy is often key to recovering from an ankle sprain because the right exercises can help rebuild flexibility and strength. Treatment for a sprained ankle depends on the severity of your injury. The treatment goals are to reduce pain and swelling, promote healing of the ligament, and restore function of the ankle. For severe injuries, you may be referred to a specialist in musculoskeletal injuries, such as an orthopedic surgeon or a physician specializing in physical medicine and rehabilitation.

Also some self-treatment methods. Rest: Stay away from activities that can lead to swelling, pain, and strain. Ice. Use an ice pack or ice slush bath immediately for 15 to 20 minutes and repeat every two to three hours while you're awake. If you have vascular disease, diabetes or decreased sensation, talk with your doctor before applying ice. Elevation. To reduce swelling, elevate your ankle above the level of your heart, especially at night. Gravity helps reduce swelling by draining excess fluid.
